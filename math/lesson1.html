1. The Power of Place Value: Where Numbers Live!

Imagine numbers living in houses on a street. Each house has a special "value" or importance based on its position. This is called place value.

Whole Numbers

In whole numbers, digits have values like ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, and so on. For example, in the number 4,567:

  • The 4 is in the thousands place, so its value is 4,000.
  • The 5 is in the hundreds place, so its value is 500.
  • The 6 is in the tens place, so its value is 60.
  • The 7 is in the ones place, so its value is 7.

Tiny Numbers, Big Ideas: Understanding Decimals

What about numbers smaller than 1? These are called decimal numbers. After the decimal point, the values become smaller: tenths, hundredths, thousandths, and so on. For example, in the number 0.005089:

  • The 5 is in the thousandths place (0.005).
  • The 0 after the 5 is in the ten-thousandths place (0.0000).
  • The 8 is in the hundred-thousandths place (0.00008).
  • The 9 is in the millionths place (0.000009).

2. Rounding Up! Making Numbers Simpler

Sometimes, we don't need super-exact numbers. That's where rounding off comes in! To round a number, you look at the digit next door to the place you are rounding to.

The "Neighbour Rule"

  • If the "neighbour" digit is 5 or more (5, 6, 7, 8, 9), you round up (add 1 to the digit you are rounding).
  • If the "neighbour" digit is less than 5 (0, 1, 2, 3, 4), you keep the digit the same.

Belizean Example: Imagine a product at a local market costs BZ$12.87. If you only deal with whole dollars, you might round it to BZ$13!

3. Rounding with Significant Figures

Significant figures are the digits in a number that are important for its precision. We use them for rounding very large or very small numbers.

Rounding Decimals to Two Significant Figures

Let's try an example: Round 0.005089 to two significant figures.

  1. Find the first non-zero digit. This is 5.
  2. Count two significant figures: 5 (first) and 0 (second).
  3. Look at the "neighbour" digit. It's 8.
  4. Since 8 is 5 or more, we round up the second significant figure (the 0). So, 0 becomes 1.
  5. So, 0.005089 rounded to two significant figures is 0.0051.

Rounding Large Numbers to Three Significant Figures

Let's try an example: Round 53,879 to three significant figures.

  1. Find the first non-zero digit. This is 5.
  2. Count three significant figures: 5, 3, 8.
  3. Look at the "neighbour" digit. It's 7.
  4. Since 7 is 5 or more, we round up the third significant figure (the 8). So, 8 becomes 9.
  5. All digits after the third significant figure become 0.
  6. So, 53,879 rounded to three significant figures is 53,900.
