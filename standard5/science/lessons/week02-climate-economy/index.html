⛓ Cause-and-Effect Chains

One minute to refresh. Then the practice begins.

The economy is the way a country earns and spends. Belize's two biggest earning sectors are tourism — visitors who come for the reef, the cayes and the rainforest — and agriculture — sugar in the north, citrus in the south, corn and beans countrywide. Climate change threatens BOTH.

A cause-and-effect chain is one event triggering the next, so a change in the sea can end as a change in a family's income. Mr. EdLo drew two of them:

Two chains, both ending at a family's income — tourism above, agriculture below.

🏖️ Tourism: warmer seas cause coral bleaching — the coral turns white and dies. Snorkellers come to see living reef, so bookings fall and hotels and guides earn less. Stronger hurricanes damage hotels and cancel flights, emptying the rooms for a whole season.

🌾 Agriculture: drought and flood both destroy a harvest — sugar in the north, citrus in Stann Creek, corn and beans everywhere. Hurricanes Eta and Iota flooded farms across the country within two weeks of each other in 2020.

Climate change here is not only "hotter days." The bigger threats are stronger hurricanes, coral bleaching, and rain that no longer comes when it is expected. Picture two real people — a hotel owner in San Pedro and a citrus farmer in Stann Creek. One bad season reaches BOTH of their pockets, by different chains.

🏖️ TOURISM: warmer sea → coral bleaches → fewer snorkellers book → hotels and guides earn less
🌾 AGRICULTURE: drought or flood → the harvest fails → less to sell → the farming family earns less

The chain does not even stop at the family. Less food reaching the market raises prices for everyone — including families who grow nothing at all.
