Section 1 · Think first

🌀 Two Hurricanes, Two Weeks

Here is one true sentence: “In 2020, hurricanes Eta and Iota flooded farms across Belize within two weeks of each other.”

Now imagine it is YOUR family:

🤔 If you were a corn farmer or a hotel owner, what would that flooding mean for you?

Both lose income. The farmer's crop drowns in the field, and the hotel owner's guests cancel their trips. Belize's two biggest earners — agriculture and tourism — both depend on the climate. This lesson traces exactly how.

Section 2 · Learn it

⛓️ Cause-and-Effect Chains

Belize's two biggest earners are tourism (visitors who come for the reef, the cayes, the jungle) and agriculture (sugar in the north, citrus in the south, corn and more). Climate change threatens BOTH — and we can trace it link by link, like a chain:

Warmer sea → coral bleaches → fewer tourists → less income.

🏖️ Tourism: warmer seas bleach the coral reef that snorkellers come to see, and stronger hurricanes damage hotels and cancel trips.

🌾 Agriculture: drought and flood cycles hurt sugar in the north, citrus in the south (like Stann Creek), and corn. Hurricanes Eta and Iota (2020) flooded farms across the country.

Think of two real people: a hotel owner in San Pedro and a citrus farmer in Stann Creek. A bad hurricane season reaches BOTH of their pockets — through different chains.
