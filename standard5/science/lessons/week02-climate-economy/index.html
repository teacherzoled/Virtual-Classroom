⛓️ Cause-and-Effect Chains

One minute to refresh. Then the practice begins.

Belize's two biggest earning sectors are tourism (visitors who come for the reef, the cayes, and the rainforest) and agriculture (sugar in the north, citrus in the south, corn and beans countrywide). Climate change threatens BOTH — and we can trace it link by link, like a chain.

A cause-and-effect chain is one event that triggers the next, so a change in the sea can end as a change in a family's income:

Warmer sea → coral bleaches → fewer tourists → less income.

🏖️ Tourism: warmer seas bleach the coral reef that snorkellers come to see, and stronger hurricanes damage hotels and cancel trips.

🌾 Agriculture: drought and flood both destroy harvests — sugar in the north, citrus in Stann Creek, and corn. Hurricanes Eta and Iota (2020) flooded farms across the country within two weeks.

Climate change here is not only "hotter days." For Belize the bigger threats are stronger hurricanes, coral bleaching, and rain that no longer comes when expected. Picture two real people — a hotel owner in San Pedro and a citrus farmer in Stann Creek — and one bad hurricane season reaches BOTH of their pockets, through different chains.

🏖️ TOURISM chain: warmer sea → coral bleaches → fewer snorkellers → hotels and guides earn less
🌾 AGRICULTURE chain: drought or flood → the harvest fails → less to sell → the farming family earns less

Care in reasoning means never stopping at the first, easiest answer. Ask each time: "And then what happens? And who does that affect?"
